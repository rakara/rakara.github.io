How to Integrate M-Pesa STK Push Into Any Website (Step-by-Step)

Rakara Abila
28 May 2026 8 min read

M-Pesa is how Kenya pays. If your website can't accept it, you're losing customers the moment they reach checkout. The Safaricom Daraja API makes integration straightforward — once you understand the flow. This guide walks you through it from scratch.

What Is STK Push?

STK Push (SIM Toolkit Push) sends a payment prompt directly to a customer's phone. They enter their M-Pesa PIN and the transaction completes. No redirects, no USSD codes — just a native phone prompt. For conversion rates, it's the gold standard.

"The best payment experience is one the customer barely notices. STK Push gets out of the way and lets the transaction happen."

Step 1 — Get Your Daraja Credentials

Head to developer.safaricom.co.ke and create an account. Once verified, create a new app and note down your Consumer Key and Consumer Secret. You'll also need a Business Shortcode (Paybill or Till number) and a Passkey from your Safaricom business account.

For testing, use the sandbox credentials Safaricom provides. They include a test shortcode (174379) and a test Passkey so you can simulate transactions without moving real money.

Step 2 — Generate an OAuth Token

Every Daraja API call requires a short-lived Bearer token. You get it by base64-encoding your Consumer Key and Secret and hitting the auth endpoint.

GET https://sandbox.safaricom.co.ke/oauth/v1/generate?grant_type=client_credentials
Authorization: Basic base64(ConsumerKey:ConsumerSecret)

Cache this token — it's valid for 3600 seconds. Fetch a fresh one only when it expires to avoid hitting rate limits.

Step 3 — Initiate the STK Push

Once you have a token, call the stkpush/v1/processrequest endpoint with a JSON payload. The key fields are the shortcode, passkey, amount, phone number, and your callback URL.

The Password field is a base64 encoding of Shortcode + Passkey + Timestamp — this is where most people trip up. Generate the timestamp in the format YYYYMMDDHHmmss in East Africa Time.

Step 4 — Handle the Callback

Safaricom posts the transaction result to the CallBackURL you supplied. Your endpoint must be publicly reachable over HTTPS (no localhost in production). Parse the ResultCode — 0 means success, anything else is a failure with a description you should log and surface to the user.

Update your database and send the customer a confirmation only after you receive a successful callback. Never trust the frontend alone.

Step 5 — Go Live

Switch the base URL from sandbox.safaricom.co.ke to api.safaricom.co.ke, swap in your production credentials, and submit a go-live request through the Daraja portal. Safaricom typically reviews within 24–48 hours on business days.

Common Pitfalls

  • Wrong phone format. Always use 254XXXXXXXXX — strip the leading 0 or +.
  • Callback URL not reachable. Use a service like ngrok during local development, and ensure your production server has a valid SSL cert.
  • Not idempotent. Safaricom can send the same callback more than once. Check your DB before processing a duplicate.
  • Hardcoded credentials. Always use environment variables. Never commit keys to version control.

That's the full picture. If you'd rather have someone else handle the integration, drop me a message — M-Pesa STK Push is one of the most common things I build for Kenyan clients.
